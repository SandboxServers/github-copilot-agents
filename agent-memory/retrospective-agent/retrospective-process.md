## Blameless Retrospective Process

### Purpose
A structured, blameless retrospective extracts maximum learning from every engagement,
sprint, or incident. The goal is continuous improvement of systems and processes, never
assigning individual blame.

### Timebox
- Sprint retrospective: 60 minutes
- Engagement retrospective: 90 minutes
- Keep to the timebox — open a follow-up session rather than running over

### Preparation (Before the Meeting)
The facilitator must prepare data before the session begins:
- Construct a timeline of key events, decisions, and milestones
- Gather metrics: velocity, deployment frequency, defect counts, cost actuals
- Collect previous retrospective action items and their current status
- Identify key decisions made during the engagement and their outcomes
- Send pre-read materials to participants 24 hours in advance
- Reserve a shared document for real-time note-taking during the session

### Facilitator Role
- Neutral party — ideally someone not deeply involved in the work being reviewed
- Keeps discussion on track and within timebox
- Ensures all voices are heard, not just the loudest
- Redirects blame-oriented language toward systemic analysis
- Captures action items with owners and due dates in real time
- Follows up on action items between retrospectives

### Ground Rules
Establish these at the start of every retrospective:
- **No blame** — focus on systems, processes, and information flow, not individuals
- **Assume positive intent** — everyone was doing their best with available information
- **Evidence over opinion** — bring data, timelines, and artifacts, not feelings
- **Equal voice** — every participant contributes, regardless of seniority
- **Confidentiality** — specifics of discussion stay in the room
- **One conversation** — no side discussions or multitasking

### Meeting Structure

#### 1. Review Previous Action Items (10 minutes)
Start every retrospective by reviewing action items from the previous session:
- Completed: validate the improvement had the intended impact
- In progress: identify blockers and adjust timelines
- Not started: escalate or explicitly deprioritize with documented reasoning

#### 2. What Went Well (15-20 minutes)
- Identify practices, decisions, and behaviors that produced good outcomes
- Be specific — "the automated infrastructure tests caught the NSG misconfiguration"
- Capture what to continue and reinforce, not just what to fix

#### 3. What Didn't Go Well (15-20 minutes)
- Identify gaps, failures, delays, and unexpected problems
- Focus on facts and evidence, not personal performance
- Ask "what about our process allowed this to happen?" not "who caused this?"

#### 4. What Should We Change (15-20 minutes)
- Propose specific, actionable changes to processes, tooling, or practices
- Prioritize changes by impact and feasibility
- Avoid vague improvements like "communicate better" — be concrete

#### 5. Capture Action Items (10 minutes)
Every action item must have:
- Clear description of what will be done
- Named owner (a person, not a team)
- Due date (a specific date, not "next sprint")
- Success criteria (how we know it's done)

### Output
- Written retrospective summary distributed to all participants within 24 hours
- Action items entered into the team's work tracking system
- Patterns and lessons learned added to the organizational knowledge base
- Follow-up date scheduled for action item review

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid
- Skipping the retrospective due to time pressure
- Letting it become a status meeting or complaint session
- Generating action items with no owners or due dates
- Never following up on previous action items
- Same facilitator every time (rotate to get fresh perspectives)
- Only discussing what went wrong (celebrate successes too)
