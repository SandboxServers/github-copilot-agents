## Retrospective Gotchas

### Purpose
These are subtle pitfalls that undermine retrospectives even when the team follows
standard practices. They often go unrecognized until the retrospective process has
already lost credibility.

### Remote Retrospectives Need Different Facilitation
In-person techniques don't translate directly to virtual settings.
- Silent brainstorming on sticky notes becomes async board contributions (Miro, FigJam, Retrium)
- Round-robin speaking order prevents dominant voices from monopolizing remote calls
- Camera-on norms help facilitators read room energy but shouldn't be forced
- Breakout rooms enable smaller-group honesty that full-team calls suppress
- Time zones complicate synchronous retros — consider async-first formats with sync wrap-up
- Remote facilitation requires more explicit structure and shorter time-boxes

### Anonymous Feedback Tools Can Enable Unconstructive Criticism
Anonymity removes accountability along with fear.
- Anonymous inputs sometimes become personal attacks or unconstructive venting
- Set explicit ground rules: feedback must be about processes and systems, not people
- Facilitator should filter or reframe unconstructive anonymous submissions before group discussion
- Consider "confidential but not anonymous" — facilitator knows the source but the group doesn't
- Balance anonymity with accountability: use anonymous for brainstorming, named for action items

### Retrospective Fatigue
Teams disengage when sessions are too frequent, too long, or too repetitive.
- Sprint retros every two weeks can feel excessive if the format never changes
- Keep sessions to 60-90 minutes maximum — shorter is better for routine retros
- Vary the format, activities, and facilitation style regularly
- Skip retros only with explicit team agreement, not management fiat
- If attendance drops, treat it as a signal — survey the team about what would make retros valuable
- Consider lightweight async retros for routine sprints, full sessions for milestones

### Cultural Differences in Feedback
Not all team members share the same norms around directness and hierarchy.
- Some cultures consider direct criticism of processes to be implicit criticism of leadership
- Hierarchical cultures may defer to senior voices even in "flat" retro formats
- Written/anonymous inputs lower the barrier for indirect communicators
- Facilitators should actively solicit input from quieter participants
- Avoid assuming silence means agreement — it may mean discomfort
- Acknowledge cultural context explicitly: "Different perspectives make retros more effective"

### Mixing Team and Individual Feedback
Retrospectives are for team and process improvement, not individual performance reviews.
- Individual performance feedback should be handled in 1:1s, not retros
- When personal friction surfaces, acknowledge it and redirect to the process layer
- "Person X didn't review PRs fast enough" becomes "Our code review process has bottlenecks"
- If individual issues dominate, the team likely has a management problem, not a retro problem
- Facilitator must redirect without dismissing: "That sounds important — let's address it in the right forum"

### Cross-Team Action Items Are Hard to Track
Action items that require changes from other teams often stall.
- Items crossing team boundaries need an explicit escalation path
- Assign an owner within the team to track the cross-team dependency
- Use shared backlogs or coordination channels — don't assume the other team knows
- If the same cross-team blocker appears in multiple retros, escalate to leadership
- Track cross-team items separately and review their status with higher visibility
- Set realistic timelines — cross-team changes take 2-3x longer than internal ones

### Metrics Gaming
Teams optimize the numbers being measured instead of actual improvement.
- If you track "number of action items completed," teams create easy action items
- If you track "cycle time," teams split work into trivially small units
- Metrics should be indicators, not targets — use them for trend analysis, not performance evaluation
- Combine quantitative metrics with qualitative team health assessments
- Watch for metrics that improve while team satisfaction or delivery quality degrades
- Goodhart's Law applies: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure
