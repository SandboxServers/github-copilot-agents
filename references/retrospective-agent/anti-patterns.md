## Retrospective Anti-Patterns

### Purpose
Recognizing anti-patterns is as important as following best practices. These patterns
undermine the value of retrospectives and erode team trust over time. When you observe
these, call them out and redirect toward constructive alternatives.

### Blame Culture
Focusing on who caused the problem instead of systemic causes.
- "Who broke the pipeline?" shuts down honesty and encourages hiding mistakes
- Blame drives defensive behavior — people stop raising issues proactively
- Root cause analysis should trace to **systems, processes, and gaps** not individuals
- Reframe: "What conditions allowed this to happen?" not "Who did this?"
- Teams that blame individuals see fewer reported near-misses over time

### No Action Items
Discussing problems without committing to concrete fixes.
- Retrospectives without action items are venting sessions, not improvement mechanisms
- Every identified problem should produce at least one actionable next step
- If the team can't identify an action, the problem needs more analysis — not deferral
- Track the ratio of issues raised to action items created as a health metric

### Action Items Without Owners
Producing "we should" statements with no individual accountability.
- "We should improve our testing" is not an action item — it's a wish
- Every action item needs a **named owner** (a person, not a team or role)
- Unowned items have near-zero completion rates
- If nobody volunteers to own it, it either isn't important enough or needs decomposition

### Never Following Up on Previous Action Items
Starting each retrospective fresh without reviewing prior commitments.
- Opens every retro by reviewing status of previous action items
- Unreviewed action items signal that the process doesn't matter
- Teams lose trust in the retrospective process when nothing changes
- Track completion rate of action items — below 60% indicates a systemic problem

### Same Format Every Time
Using the same retrospective structure for every session.
- Teams disengage when the format becomes predictable and stale
- Rotate between formats: Start/Stop/Continue, 4Ls, Timeline, Sailboat, Lean Coffee
- Match the format to the context — incident postmortems need different structure than sprint retros
- Novelty in format encourages fresh thinking and broader participation

### Only Holding Retros After Failures
Treating retrospectives as incident response rather than continuous improvement.
- Retros triggered only by failure associate the process with negativity
- Successful deliveries also have lessons — what went right and why
- Regular cadence (every sprint, every engagement phase) normalizes the practice
- Celebrating successes builds the psychological safety needed to discuss failures

### Too Many Action Items
Trying to fix everything in a single retrospective cycle.
- Limit to 2-3 action items per retrospective — focus enables completion
- Prioritize by impact: what one change would produce the most improvement?
- Excess action items overwhelm the team and dilute accountability
- Better to complete 3 items fully than start 10 and finish none
- Use a parking lot for lower-priority items to revisit in future retros

### Retrospective as Unstructured Venting
Allowing the session to become an unfacilitated complaint session.
- Without facilitation, dominant voices control the conversation
- Structured activities (silent brainstorming, dot voting, timeboxed rounds) ensure equity
- The facilitator's role is to keep discussion constructive and forward-looking
- Validate frustrations, then redirect: "What would need to change to prevent this?"

### Management Attendance Causing Self-Censorship
Having managers or leadership present when the team needs candid discussion.
- People self-censor when their manager is in the room
- Run a safety check at the start — if safety is low, adjust the format
- Consider manager-free retros with a summary shared afterward
- If managers attend, they should explicitly take a listening-only role
- Separate team retrospectives from leadership retrospectives

### Skipping Retros When Things Went Well
Canceling retrospectives when there are no obvious problems.
- "Everything went fine" usually means issues went unnoticed, not that none exist
- Good outcomes often mask inefficiencies or unsustainable practices
- Retros during calm periods are ideal for proactive improvement
- Use success retros to document what worked so it can be replicated

### Not Tracking Metrics Over Time
Failing to capture and trend improvement data across retrospectives.
- Without metrics, you can't demonstrate that retrospectives drive improvement
- Track: action item completion rate, recurring issue frequency, DORA metrics trends
- Compare themes across retros — are the same problems surfacing repeatedly?
- Use trend data to make the case for organizational investment in fixes

### Recency Bias
Only discussing events from the last few days, missing systemic patterns.
- Teams naturally focus on the most recent incidents or frustrations
- Use timelines or engagement logs to ensure the full period is covered
- Prompt for early-phase issues: "What happened in the first week that shaped the rest?"
- Cross-reference with prior retro themes to identify recurring patterns
- Recency bias causes teams to solve symptoms rather than root causes
